Diabetes mellitus is a metabolic disorder characterized by persistent hyperglycemia due to insulin deficiency or resistance.
Symptoms
Frequent urination (polyuria) is a common early symptom of diabetes.
Excessive thirst (polydipsia) occurs due to fluid loss from increased urination.
Unexplained weight loss may occur despite normal or increased appetite.
Persistent fatigue is often reported due to impaired glucose utilization.
Blurred vision may occur when blood glucose levels fluctuate significantly.
Slow wound healing is associated with poor glycemic control.
Recurrent infections, including skin and urinary infections, may indicate diabetes.
Diagnosis
Diabetes is diagnosed if fasting blood glucose is ≥126 mg/dL on two separate occasions.
HbA1c ≥6.5% confirms the diagnosis of diabetes.
A random blood glucose level ≥200 mg/dL with symptoms indicates diabetes.
An oral glucose tolerance test (OGTT) showing ≥200 mg/dL after 2 hours confirms diabetes.
Prediabetes is indicated by fasting glucose between 100–125 mg/dL or HbA1c between 5.7–6.4%.
Treatment
Lifestyle modification is mandatory and includes dietary control and regular physical activity.
Patients should reduce intake of refined sugars and high-glycemic foods.
Weight management is recommended for overweight individuals.
Metformin is the first-line pharmacological treatment unless contraindicated.
Additional oral agents may be prescribed if glycemic targets are not achieved.
Insulin therapy is required when oral medications are insufficient or contraindicated.
Blood glucose monitoring should be performed regularly.
HbA1c should be monitored every 3–6 months.
Complications
Chronic hyperglycemia may lead to diabetic neuropathy.
Diabetic nephropathy may result in progressive kidney damage.
Diabetic retinopathy can lead to vision impairment or blindness.
Cardiovascular disease risk is significantly increased in diabetic patients.
Acute complications include diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA).
Severe hypoglycemia may occur due to overtreatment.
