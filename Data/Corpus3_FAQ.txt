What is diabetes?
Diabetes is a chronic condition in which blood glucose levels remain elevated due to problems with insulin production or function.

What are the early symptoms of diabetes?
Early symptoms include frequent urination, excessive thirst, fatigue, unexplained weight loss, blurred vision, and slow wound healing. Some individuals may also experience recurrent infections.

How is diabetes diagnosed?
Diabetes is diagnosed through blood tests. A fasting blood glucose level of 126 mg/dL or higher on two occasions confirms diabetes. An HbA1c value of 6.5% or above is also diagnostic. A random blood glucose level above 200 mg/dL with symptoms may indicate diabetes. An oral glucose tolerance test may also be used.

What is prediabetes?
Prediabetes is a condition where blood glucose levels are higher than normal but not high enough to be classified as diabetes. It is indicated by fasting glucose between 100–125 mg/dL or HbA1c between 5.7–6.4%.

How is diabetes managed?
Management includes lifestyle changes such as improving diet, increasing physical activity, and maintaining a healthy weight. These steps help control blood glucose levels.

What medications are used for diabetes?
Metformin is commonly used as the first-line treatment. If blood glucose levels remain uncontrolled, additional oral medications or insulin therapy may be required.

How often should monitoring be done?
Blood glucose should be monitored regularly. HbA1c levels are typically checked every 3 to 6 months to assess long-term control.

What complications can occur?
Diabetes can lead to complications such as nerve damage, kidney disease, eye problems, and cardiovascular disease. Acute complications like diabetic ketoacidosis can also occur.

Can diabetes cause low blood sugar?
Yes, hypoglycemia can occur, especially in patients taking insulin or certain medications. Symptoms include dizziness, sweating, and confusion.
