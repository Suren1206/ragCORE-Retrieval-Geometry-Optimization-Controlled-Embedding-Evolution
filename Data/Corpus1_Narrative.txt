Diabetes mellitus is a chronic metabolic condition in which the body is unable to effectively regulate blood glucose levels. This may occur due to insufficient insulin production or resistance to insulin action. Over time, elevated glucose levels can have widespread effects on various organ systems.

The early symptoms of diabetes often develop gradually and may go unnoticed. Individuals commonly experience increased thirst and frequent urination as the body attempts to eliminate excess glucose through urine. Fatigue is another frequent complaint, resulting from the body’s inability to efficiently utilize glucose for energy. Some patients may also report unexplained weight loss, blurred vision, or delayed wound healing. Recurrent infections, particularly of the skin or urinary tract, can also be indicative of underlying diabetes.

Diagnosis is established through laboratory testing. A fasting blood glucose level of 126 mg/dL or higher on two separate occasions is considered diagnostic. Alternatively, an HbA1c value of 6.5% or above reflects sustained elevated glucose levels over time. In symptomatic individuals, a random blood glucose level exceeding 200 mg/dL may also confirm the diagnosis. Additional tests such as the oral glucose tolerance test may be used in certain cases to assess glucose metabolism more thoroughly.

Management of diabetes involves a combination of lifestyle and pharmacological interventions. Patients are advised to adopt a balanced diet, limit intake of high-glycemic foods, and engage in regular physical activity. Weight reduction is particularly important for individuals with insulin resistance. Metformin is commonly prescribed as the first-line medication, while additional oral agents may be added if glycemic control is not achieved. In more advanced cases, insulin therapy may be required.

If diabetes is not adequately controlled, it can lead to both acute and chronic complications. Long-term complications include damage to nerves, kidneys, and eyes, collectively referred to as microvascular complications. Additionally, individuals with diabetes are at a significantly higher risk of cardiovascular disease. Acute complications, such as diabetic ketoacidosis, can develop rapidly and require immediate medical intervention. Episodes of hypoglycemia may also occur, particularly in patients receiving intensive treatment.
